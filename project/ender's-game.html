A personification of empathy.

You're probably familiar with Ender's Game. You're probably not familiar with the "18 novels, 16 short stories, 2 short story anthologies, 1 collection of essays, 13 comic series and one-shots, 1 manga, 1 film, 1 reference book, and 1 audioplay adaptation" just from Ender's universe. Personally, I've only read most of Ender's books, as well as "Saints" by Orson Scott Card (not to be confused with "Saints"). Ender is deliberately written as the most empathetic living person, which makes his books into schools of virtue. His characters think in ways I've never thought before, silently soliloquizing brief essays on the most beautiful and fascinating topics. Don't fear boredom, though, my first read-through of Ender's Game was an all-nighter on a cot in a tiny unfinished house my dad was renting during my parent's divorce. Despite repeated admonitions, I found sources of light to read by so I could learn what Ender did about the buggers. I read it twice that week. I was thirteen.

Ender embodies our people.

Ender is strong, defiant and undefeatable. Ender knows how to better his friends, and loves them unconditionally. He never loses hope, he never fails to save his people or find a purpose. Get over the strange ideas and look to the root of the message to see a defense of our faith. For instance, during the Shadow of the Hegemon, an atheist travels with a nun, who makes some of the best arguments for Christianity in literary fiction. Then, he meets a member of our church, who teaches him that family is the greatest purpose. Then a gay scientist explains why he's marrying a woman and raising her children. Ultimately, Bean decides that marrying the woman he loves and having children with her is worth the risk that his damaged genes pose. Astonishingly, none of this feels forced. In fact, it is so subtle that my brother read it and thought the atheism of Bean was a repudiation of our religion.
